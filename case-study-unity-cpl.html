01 — The Problem

A process held together
by email and assumption.

Unity Environmental University offers students the ability to earn academic credit for professional experience, internships, and uncertified training through a Credit for Prior Learning (CPL) Portfolio. The idea is sound — students with real-world expertise shouldn't have to repeat learning they've already demonstrated. The process for accessing that credit was not.

Students could upload documents to a general repository, but the uploads had no organization, no context, and no connection to any specific course. After uploading, the student then had to track down the Dean, initiate a conversation to explain which course they wanted to challenge and why, learn the requirements for that course's portfolio, and pay a fee through a third-party system that existed entirely outside the portal. There were no notifications, no guidance, no status visibility, and no confirmation that anything was moving forward.

"A student who had the experience to earn credit had to navigate a process that assumed they already knew how it worked."

Before

  • Unorganized document uploads to a general repository
  • No connection between uploaded files and a specific course
  • Student had to manually contact the Dean to initiate review
  • Learning outcomes not surfaced — students guessed at requirements
  • Payment via a separate third-party system outside the portal
  • No notifications triggered on either end of the process
  • No status visibility — students waited with no feedback
  • Entirely manual on the administrative side

After

  • Structured 3-step portfolio builder inside the Student Portal
  • Course search connects portfolio directly to the challenged course
  • Learning outcomes surfaced from the course — no guessing
  • Students address each learning outcome with direct written responses
  • Specific file references linked to each learning outcome response
  • Payment at time of submission or deferred with clear conditions
  • Portfolio status visible on dashboard with next-step guidance
  • Notifications triggered at each stage change

02 — Strategic Context

One feature at a time.
Each one pulling infrastructure with it.

The CPL Portfolio feature is part of the same staged rollout strategy that governs all new Student Portal features at Unity — design within the new but not-yet-deployed portal, then release one feature at a time rather than waiting for the full portal to be complete.

What makes this feature strategically more complex than the ADA Accommodations workflow is the technical dependency it creates. To surface learning outcomes from a challenged course, the portal needs a functioning course search. I had already designed a robust course search as part of the forthcoming Course Registration tool — but that tool wasn't ready for deployment. The solution was deliberate: strip the course search down to the functionality needed for CPL, deploy it as part of this rollout, and document the intended scale-up for development so they could build with the more complete version in mind from the beginning.

The Infrastructure Strategy

Rather than blocking the CPL feature until Course Registration was ready, or building a throwaway course search just for CPL, the decision was to deploy a simplified version of the designed course search and communicate the full intended architecture to the development team in parallel. This gives students a working feature now, gives developers the context to build toward the complete version, and avoids the technical debt of a disconnected implementation. Each feature rollout is also a foundation for the next one.

The CPL Portfolio also introduces the Credit Management section — a new top-level navigation area that will eventually house both CPL and Transfer Evaluation under a unified framework. The Combined Transfer Credits & Credit for Prior Learning Progress bar at the top of Credit Management gives students a visual understanding of how their prior learning credits and transfer credits combine toward their degree — a transparency feature that didn't exist before and that required designing a new data visualization component for the portal.

03 — Discovery & Process

Workshops, best-case-first,
and a design decision that went beyond the ask.

The discovery and design process for the CPL Portfolio followed the same best-case-first methodology used for the ADA Accommodations feature — design the ideal experience first, then reality-check it through direct stakeholder engagement. The process was more complex here because the stakeholder group was larger.

1

Map the current process

Documented every step of the existing patchwork workflow — document uploads, manual Dean outreach, third-party payment, absence of notifications — to make the full friction surface visible before designing anything.

2

Best-case-scenario first pass

Designed the ideal CPL Portfolio experience in three steps: document upload with drag-and-drop and cloud storage link support (Step 1), course search with learning outcomes (Step 2), and review and submit with integrated payment (Step 3).

3

1-on-1 workshop with Megann (Assistant Dean of Student Success)

Walked through the rough wireframes step by step with the person who handles all CPL cases day to day. This session surfaced operational details and generated the working specification that drove subsequent development and stakeholder conversations.

4

Expanded review — Doreen (VP of Student Outcomes)

Once the design reached a solid working state, brought in the primary decision-maker. Used Adobe Acrobat and Adobe Cloud for commenting on screenshots — no account creation required for stakeholders, removing a barrier to participation.

5

Async stakeholder review — Michaeleen (Dean of Baccalaureate Programs)

Multiple decision-makers meant not all review could happen synchronously. Adobe Cloud commenting enabled Michaeleen and others to review and respond on their own timeline, with Megann consolidating feedback from behind-the-scenes conversations.

6

Major structural change — Step order reversed

The consolidated stakeholder feedback produced one significant change: Challenge a Course (originally Step 2) needed to move to Step 1, so that Learning Outcomes — newly added — were surfaced earlier in the process. Document upload moved to Step 2. The design rationale was sound: students need to know what they're submitting against before they organize and upload their evidence.

7

Design insight — going beyond what stakeholders asked for

Stakeholders were satisfied to display learning outcomes clearly. Further investigation revealed that students are required to specifically address each learning outcome in their portfolio — not just submit documents generally. This insight drove a design decision not requested by stakeholders: required text fields under each learning outcome, carried from the course selection in Step 1, so students could directly summarize how their portfolio demonstrates each outcome and reference specific files by name.

The Insight Moment

The difference between showing learning outcomes and requiring students to address them is the difference between a transparent process and a guided one. Stakeholders knew what the requirements were. Students didn't. The design decision to add per-outcome text fields wasn't in scope — it came from asking one more question about how the process actually works. That kind of investigation is what separates a feature that functions from one that actually helps.

04 — The Solution

Three steps.
One complete guided experience.

The redesigned CPL Portfolio brings the entire process into the Student Portal — course selection, learning outcome mapping, document upload, outcome addressing, review, and payment — in a structured three-step flow that gives students clear guidance at every point.

1
Challenge a Course

The student searches for and selects the course they are challenging with their prior learning. The course search — a simplified version of the fuller Course Registration search — surfaces the course name, description, credits, and critically, the learning outcomes the student's portfolio must demonstrate.

Moving this to Step 1 was the key structural insight: students need to know what they're submitting against before they organize their evidence. The learning outcomes selected here carry forward into Step 2, where students must respond to each one directly.

What it surfaces

Course name, credit value, course description, and the full list of learning outcomes the portfolio must address — pulled from the course database and displayed before any documents are uploaded.

2
Upload Portfolio Documents

The student uploads their portfolio evidence using a drag-and-drop interface (or click to upload), with file type restrictions, maximum file size, and a cloud storage share link option for large files. A callout box reiterates the required documents list so students can verify completeness before proceeding.

Below the upload zone, each learning outcome from Step 1 is displayed with a required text field. Students must write a direct summary of how their portfolio demonstrates that specific outcome and reference the specific file names where it is covered. This field is required — the portfolio cannot be submitted without addressing each outcome.

The design decision beyond the ask

Stakeholders requested that learning outcomes be displayed clearly. Investigation revealed students are required to address each one specifically. The text fields were added — not requested — because the requirement existed whether or not the design surfaced it. Without the fields, students would still be expected to do this work; they just wouldn't know how or where.

3
Review & Submit

The student reviews their complete submission: the challenged course, the uploaded files, and their learning outcome responses. A clear notice explains the non-refundable processing fee before submission. Once submitted, the student is given the choice to pay immediately or defer payment — with full transparency into the consequences of deferral.

Review includes

Challenged course name and learning outcomes, uploaded file list, learning outcome written responses, and the non-refundable fee disclosure before the final submit action.

05 — The Design Decisions

The details that
don't show in a screenshot.

Learning outcomes as a required input, not a display

The most consequential design decision in this project wasn't in scope. Stakeholders asked for learning outcomes to be displayed clearly — a reasonable ask that would have improved on the previous process. But displaying requirements and requiring responses to them are different design choices with very different outcomes for students.

A student who reads the learning outcomes and then uploads a folder of documents is doing the same thing they were doing before — just with more information. A student who is required to write one to two sentences directly addressing each learning outcome, and reference the specific files where that outcome is demonstrated, is doing something materially different: they are constructing an argument, not just submitting evidence. That distinction matters to the Dean who reviews the portfolio and to the student who needs to understand what the portfolio is actually for.

Cloud storage links alongside file upload

Video files, large design portfolios, and multimedia work samples frequently exceed file size limits for standard upload interfaces. Adding a cloud storage share link option alongside the drag-and-drop uploader means students with large or complex portfolios don't have to compress or fragment their work to fit the form. The requirement was the same; the accommodation was the form adapting to the reality of what students actually submit.

Payment deferral with explicit conditions

Offering payment deferral was a student-centered decision — not every student has the processing fee available at the moment they finish their portfolio. But deferral without clear conditions would have created a worse problem: portfolios sitting in limbo without students understanding why nothing was moving. The design shows the deferral option but requires students to read and acknowledge the conditions — 60-day expiry, no review until payment received — before confirming. Transparency at the point of decision is less friction than confusion after the fact.

The course search as a deliberate partial deployment

The course search embedded in Step 1 is a simplified version of a more robust search designed for the forthcoming Course Registration feature. Rather than building a throwaway implementation or blocking CPL on Course Registration's timeline, the decision was to deploy the simplified version with development documentation describing the intended full architecture. Students get working functionality now. Developers build with the complete picture in mind from the start. The foundation doesn't need to be rebuilt later.

06 — Reflection

What I'd do differently.

Honest retrospective

The multi-stakeholder review process worked well — the combination of a 1-on-1 workshop with Megann, a joint session with Doreen, and async Adobe Cloud commenting for Michaeleen was the right approach for a group with different schedules and decision-making authority. The async tool choice specifically removed the account-creation barrier that would have slowed things down. I'd use this structure again.

The learning outcome text fields were the right call, but they came from my own investigation rather than a structured research question. If I were doing this again, I'd add one question to the initial stakeholder conversation: "What does the Dean actually review when they evaluate a portfolio?" That question would have surfaced the outcome-addressing requirement in session one rather than in a later investigation, and could have shaped the Step 1 → Step 2 reorder decision from the start.

The payment deferral flow is clear, but the 60-day expiry countdown on the dashboard is a design element I'd want to user-test before finalizing. A countdown timer can feel urgent and helpful or stressful and arbitrary depending on how it's presented. Without observational data from students under real conditions, the optimal framing for that element remains an assumption.

The feature is designed and awaiting implementation. The case study will be updated with outcome data — portfolio completion rates, payment deferral rates, and Dean review turnaround time — once the feature is live and generating signal.
